Project Overview

Project Description

This project will focus on designing and developing a new platform for providing/organizing midyear/annual review feedback. The current process of emailing feedback to managers (shown below) leads to a lot of time and effort spent organizing/formatting the feedback. This stems from emails being easily missed, differing format of feedback sent (Google Doc/pasted in Email/Word Doc…etc). We want to be able to streamline this process and allow managers to focus on the quality and delivering valuable feedback rather than organizing it all out.

Current Process

Side note: This past review period (June 2017) was scheduled to take two weeks, but ended up lasting 5 weeks due to unmet deadlines and more time/effort spent organizing feedback than expected.

Key Results

  • Decrease number of days over first deadline dealing with feedback requests
  • Decrease number of days over review period where feedback is still not delivered/submitted
  • Decrease number of hours spent approving and organizing feedback
  • Improve quality of feedback. Feedback should be actionable, specific, and kind. (Secondary Goal)

People Problem and Goals

What’s the Value Added?

Cut down time and effort spent organizing and reminding employees to write feedback. Overall reduce the amount of time spent not related to work or providing meaningful feedback.

In addition, reviews are scheduled to finish during the planning period between H1 and H2 so that employees are more available to respond and interpret their strengths and weaknesses for the coming half. The longer that feedback is not delivered the less pressing and focused it will become.

Outgoing Requests

The main thing to think about with outgoing requests involved searching for peers to write your feedback. With this search UI, I looked at 3 main things:

  • Indication that you needed 3 people to write your reviews
  • Knowledge of who is available to write or not (still available, but should show some sort of status)
  • Know the status of a request sent out

Search UI

Three Search Fields

  • Pros: The UI helps imply what is needed (3 requested peers). Gives off a sense of urgency and need for completion.
  • Cons: New BuzzFeed pattern. Space consuming and takes away from Incoming Requests. May not be entirely clear with cases of rejected requests.

Single Search Field

  • Pros: Common BuzzFeed pattern (used in SodaShop).
  • Cons: UI does not help indicate what you have to do. Initial assumption is to make one request

Search Modal

  • Pros: Makes the task of requesting peers more personable and separate. More room for detail and acts as verification to ensure user is requesting the right person.
  • Cons: UI does not help indicate the action. Introducing modals which may be foreign throughout the rest of the application.

Request Views

  • Primary and Secondary Buttoned : Quick view of all incoming requests and is easily scannable. Primary buttons add a sense of urgency, but does not draw too much attention from the rest of the screen. Places the priority on outgoing request.
  • Horizontal Cards: Does a good job of placing a lot of attention on the request itself, however looks odd in comparison with the horizontal search bars in outgoing requests. Unable to show the quantity of requests past three.
  • List with Face Profiles: Places a face with the request making it more personable and urgent. Avatar element looks out of place with the rest of the page. Possibility to look back at this and incorporate it if profiles are used elsewhere.

Button Feedback and User Guidance

Next, I thought about what the feedback for accepting a feedback request would look like. Specifically, I was looking at the two explorations below, and whether it made sense to connect accepting a request directly to writing that respective person’s feedback.

Write Feedback CTA: This might incite users to start writing their feedback right away and lead to completing written feedback sooner. Additionally, a direct CTA to writing feedback aligns with the mental model of what would happen next; similar to how accepting a request on Facebook or Instagram leads you to next view their profile.

Simple Feedback Message: The intention here was to more prominently differentiate the tasks for managing requests and writing feedback. Specifically, so that users would not get distracted and shift their focus to writing feedback rather than responding to the next request.

Requests — Final Design

Writing Feedback Page

Next, I started working on the process of writing feedback. Specifically, I looked back at my user research and noted some insights I got about how people completed this task.

  • Users rarely wrote their feedback in the email template. Each person had a different platform for recording their reviews and would often copy and paste their content into an email.
  • People often forgot who exactly they were responsible in writing feedback for and would dig back through individual emails to remember.
  • People often would write a review and then review it again later before submitting. This often led to disorganization of who they still had to write for/the progress of completion for each person.

Page Structure

Starting with the page structure, I explored two main structures shown below. The first was a list view which separated the list of people a user has to write for and the writing form while the second combined the list and form together as a single page.

  • List → Writing Page: Focus is on the writing and each person gets more attention on their feedback. Writing without distraction
  • Sidebar: Easier to monitor progress of all writing responsibilities. Considering this application is built primarily for managers, who tend to have more people to write for, being able to check progress of all responsibilities makes more sense.
  • Dropdown: The focus is on the form itself while still easily able to switch between writing responsibilities. Harder to monitor progress which may incentivize user to focus on the task at hand.

Post Mortem

At the point, the 4 week timeline for this project was up and I was ready to move onto a new team. At this point, it also gave Dave Mauro (Engineering Lead) time to catch up on the technical needs for this project.

Coming into this project, my main objective was to kick off and scope out this project until H2 planning was complete. From here, I created a handoff document to pass on to the next designer after I left my internship. There was no immediate plan to complete this project right away which meant writing a detailed document once it got picked up again in 2 months.

In doing so, I spent roughly half a week communicating with Cap and the rest of the team and organizing files based on BuzzFeed’s handoff protocol.

Lessons Learned

Understand the Product and Process Problem — This project left me on my own for the most part and had me learn a lot about product, data, and visual process altogether. Without a designated PM to help scope out the project or Data Scientist to validate assumptions against, I found myself learning a lot form the people around me. This taught me a lot about mentorship and communication in terms of being comfortable around those that you work with. Throughout these first four weeks, I gained insights into other product roles and established so many connections that helped set my goals and expedite process for the remainder of my internship.

Trust in My Own Visual Design — As this was my first project coming into this internship, I was still learning the BuzzFeed design critique process and picking up on things I noticed from other designers. Many of the other projects going on were built on established products though, so there was a larger emphasis on visual design and brand alignment especially with things related to site experience. For the first two weeks, I applied the same process to this product even though it was in a complete different scope which caused me to spend more effort than necessary on visual over interaction. Iterating past the first portion of the request page, I began to trust my own visual design decisions more and more and felt comfortable going in one direction straightaway.
