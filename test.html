üí™ The Challenge

Design a standalone bill splitting app for iOS or Android. This should include the ability to split bill and tip with multiple people as well as specify tip amount or percentage (ex. 10%, 15%, 20%).

ü§î Initial Questions

I listed out the main questions I want to answer. These were helpful not only in developing an initial understanding of the challenge, but also as a reference point to guide my ideation process throughout the design process.

  • What is the problem I am addressing?
  • Who am I designing for?
  • What are the current pain points involved with the current solution?
  • What‚Äôs the value proposition for this product?

Providing Context

üòß People Problem

Before jumping into the flows and pixels of the problem, I laid out what my understanding of the problem was and came up with the following initial problem statement:

Solutions like Venmo and hard cash are difficult to manage with a large group and I am often frustrated by how long it takes to: 1. Get my money back after paying for a meal and 2. Calculate out the cost of what I ate in the context of a large receipt.

üéñÔ∏è Product Goals

But more than just solve a problem, I envisioned this solution as a product someone would want to download and thought about what success would look like for this application.

  1. Keep users coming back (What can we offer the users past a simple calculator?)
    1. Instagram is a sharing platform as well as a photo editor; Venmo is a social feed as well as a payment tool; Snapchat is a news curator as well as a messaging platform; What can this product offer in addition to splitting the bill?
  2. Become a go to solution for splitting the bill at the end of a meal
    1. The ~dream~ is turning a brand name into a verb the same way we Google things, Venmo someone, Lyft somewhere. How can this solution be synonymous with splitting a dining check?

üôÖ‚Äç‚ôÄÔ∏è Constraints and Assumptions

Having gone through just this initial phase, I started to understand the complexity of the problem. I realized there were things that I might not get to right away and listed out some constraints and assumptions to restrain myself from diving too deep into certain topics.

  1. User Data - Will this pull profile data from Facebook in order to cut down the friction of adding someone? And how available are people‚Äôs most relevant connections to each other?
  2. Recognition Technology - Will implementing the ability to recognize something such as voice, images, location‚Ä¶etc. be as accurate and seamless as possible?
  3. Product Reach - Not everyone will have this application downloaded let alone own a smartphone. The idea here is to first get the hero right first and product reach as the secondary goal.

Understanding Users

üìí User Research Plan

I wanted to better understand the problem past my own experiences so I recruited 15 different people (6 graduate students, 5 passerby's at a coffee shop, 4 full-time employees) and walked each of them through a semi structured interview with the following research questions:

  1. What is the demographic of people that split the bill most often?
  2. Why do users choose to split the bill?
  3. What are current methods of splitting the bill now and why do users prefer those methods?
  4. What are users feeling emotionally through the different steps of splitting the bill?

In addition to formal interviews, I also recruited 2 different groups of friends to go out to dinner on in anticipation that the check would be split*. After the experience of paying the bill, I walked each friend through a series of questions as well as asking them to recount how they felt through each part of the payment process.

*this tied in nicely with the fact that I had run out of groceries

üî¨ Research Insights

Finally, I combined all my research into three insights I would use to guide my design moving forward. These helped me make some of the more difficult design decisions and ideate on different design directions.

  1. Avoiding Monetary Conflict: Money is a sensitive subject, and when possible, we avoid talking about it especially when it comes to how much someone is owed.

Usually when someone Venmo‚Äôs me, its a rough estimate or usually a little more than what I‚Äôm owed. There‚Äôs some sort of taboo associated with owing someone money and it‚Äôs just a little uncomfortable to confront someone about it ‚Äî UW Grad Student

  1. Dining as an Intimate Experience: Usually when people go out to eat, it is with people they know well already and feel comfortable around. Frequently, it‚Äôs the same or some sort of denomination of people together.

When I go out to eat, it‚Äôs usually with people I know pretty well already. I have one main group of friends that I usually go out with. It changes up a bit sometimes, but its pretty much the same people to be honest. ‚Äî Full-Time QA at Expedia

  1. Tipping as Etiquette: Tipping is more of an etiquette rather than a true indication of a server‚Äôs or restaurant‚Äôs sense of hospitality. Most people end up tipping the same amount (15-20%) or have no preference as to how much the person fronting the bill pays.

Honestly, [when the bill is split into multiple cards] I take a peek at other people‚Äôs bills and see how much they‚Äôre tipping first. I‚Äôm not good at mental math so calculating that out isn‚Äôt something I really want to do ‚Äî Full-Time Financial Analyst at Microsoft

Ultimately, I decided on `**User Flow A (Group Focus)**` because I wanted the first thing users saw was a list of their friends. This exploration places more emphasis on the interpersonal aspect of it which I believe is the more relevant value proposition over the utility of splitting a bill.

Design

Having laid out all the information I needed, I started working on how to visually show each part of the process. This involved lots of explorations and referencing my insights/interaction goals to help make certain design decisions

üñçÔ∏è Medium Fidelity Explorations

Next, I created medium fidelity mockups to explore higher level things such as list vs. grid layouts, entry/exit points, information hierarchy‚Ä¶etc.

Below are some of the main design decisions I made and rationale for my selected solution.

  • Entry Point (Application Home)

    • **Bottom Nav CTA** `` put the most emphasis on the action and would allow for other features to be placed up front such as friends, discovery‚Ä¶etc. I discussed how this application should be more than just a pure bill splitter so a bottom nav CTA allows for this.
    • `**Top Nav CTA**` made to feel quick like composing a message, but felt too similar to venmo and also de-prioritizes splitting the bill
    • `**In-Feed CTA**` associates the act of splitting the bill with the social feed, but is not accessible once feed is scrolled through

  • Feed Card Layout (Application Home)

    • **Small Group Profile Pictures** `` puts the focus on the group rather than the individual. Smaller icons also make sense in this context because these people are mainly your closer friends and therefore more easily recognizable
    • `**Single Person + Large Image**` focus is on the individual and their sense of taste in food and ability to take a quality picture. However, this would be limited to just the poster being highlighted as opposed to everyone else
    • `**Single Person + Larger Image**` focus is on the image, but makes the focus of this application food pictures rather than the ability to split a bill

  • Paying Someone vs. Requesting Payment (After Linking Food to Friends)

In research, I observed two main methods for splitting the bill including the person paying either calculate request venmo funds themselves or allocate the responsibility of paying them back to each person.

If I pay, can you guys do the math to figure out how much you owe and pay me?

I wanted to design for both these scenarios and followed a similar model to Venmo‚Äôs Request/Pay model but looked at different ways of confirmation. This was important because the person splitting the bill is responsible for directing payment requests to other people on behalf of the person paying the bill.

  • **Paying the 'Fronter'** / **Requesting Money From Other People** : I opted for these solutions as it gave the user all the information up front and followed the same model of horizontal scroll. Originally, I wanted to dismiss the ‚ÄòRecipient Overview‚Äô so that the issue of money confrontation (first research insight) would not be as relevant and in the users face. Ultimately though, I felt that with something as serious as giving someone money, it made the most sense to supply as much information as possible

  • `**Top Nav CTA**` made to feel quick like composing a message, but felt too similar to venmo and also de-prioritizes splitting the bill
  • `**In-Feed CTA**` associates the act of splitting the bill with the social feed, but is not accessible once feed is scrolled through

üî§ Copy Document

With this prototype, I looked at the wording of specific instructions and actions. I created a copy document of all the relevant vocabulary in each step and narrowed down what made the most sense.

For example, I initially struggled with how to properly differentiate between requests and payments to someone else.

  • Requests was the case where you paid, and you are splitting the bill yourself (and therefore requesting people to pay you. On the other hand, payments to someone else meant that while you might have paid, you allocated the responsibility of splitting the bill to someone else.

Ultimately, I settled on ``**Request Payment** *and * **Someone Else Paid** after quickly testing with with a few different people on what they thought would happen next.

Another great example of how this copy document came into play was how I would list out different food items (i.e **Super Burrito**` `` vs.Super Burrito ($12.25)` ``). Coming from a hospitality/restaurant background, I knew that menu designs typically don‚Äôt place dashes/parentheses around dollar amounts to avoid drawing attention to them. This document helped me visualize this and make that design decision

Final Considerations

üìè Edge Cases

  • Product Reach

    • What if somebody doesn‚Äôt have this app? or even a smartphone? How will they be notified or reminded of how much they owe someone? What would the interaction be for the person splitting the bill to search for, say, a phone number. And also, what would that notification look like for someone without the application?

  • Further explore manual entry

    • What happens if a user‚Äôs camera is broken or not able to accurately analyze what is on a bill? What kind of error would it give the user? How valuable is this edge of the user base?

üîú Next Steps

  1. Further explore the above edge cases I thought about the concept of using text messages or email as another way to request payment but would want to explore this option a bit more. Also how would manual input work? would it be possible to pull menu data from the restaurant to make this process easier? And if so what might that look like?

  2. Usability Testing Next, I would start looking for ways to test the usability of this solution and look look at how long it would take for a user to successfully split a bill, how often users step back in the process, and how people perceive the social aspect of this application.
